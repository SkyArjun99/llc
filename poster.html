Quantum Computing & Superposition

Exploring the Fundamental Principles of Quantum Information

1. Classical Bit vs Qubit

|0⟩ or |1⟩
Classical Bit
α|0⟩ + β|1⟩
Qubit
|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩
While classical bits are strictly 0 or 1, qubits exist in superposition—a quantum combination of both states simultaneously. The probabilities |α|² and |β|² represent the likelihood of measuring 0 or 1.

2. Bloch Sphere Representation

The Bloch sphere provides a 3D visualization of qubit states. The north pole represents |0⟩, south pole |1⟩, and the equator represents equal superposition states. Any point on the sphere corresponds to a valid qubit state.

3. Quantum Superposition

|ψ⟩ = 1/√2(|0⟩ + |1⟩)
Superposition allows qubits to exist in multiple states simultaneously. This fundamental principle enables quantum parallelism—processing many possibilities at once. The wave-like nature represents probability amplitudes before measurement.

4. Quantum Entanglement

|Φ⁺⟩ = 1/√2(|00⟩ + |11⟩)
Entangled qubits share a quantum state regardless of distance. Measuring one instantly affects the other. Bell states represent maximally entangled two-qubit systems, forming the basis for quantum teleportation and superdense coding.

5. Quantum Gates as Rotations

X
H
Z
Quantum gates perform rotations on the Bloch sphere:
X-gate: Bit-flip (180° rotation about X-axis)
H-gate: Hadamard (creates superposition, 90° about X+Z)
Z-gate: Phase-flip (180° rotation about Z-axis)

6. Classical vs Quantum Computing

Property Classical Quantum
Basic Unit Bit (0 or 1) Qubit (superposition)
States 2ⁿ combinations 2ⁿ simultaneously
Operations Logic gates Unitary rotations
Key Advantage Deterministic Quantum parallelism
Quantum computers leverage superposition and entanglement to explore multiple solution paths simultaneously, offering exponential speedup for specific problems like factoring, optimization, and quantum simulation.
